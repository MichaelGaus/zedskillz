    11→/**
    12→ * Extract the topic from a user question like "what is a metaphor" or "explain photosynthesis".
    13→ * Strips common question prefixes and returns the core topic.
    14→ */
    15→function extractTopic(question: string): string {
    16→  const lower = question.toLowerCase().trim();
    17→  // Remove common prefixes
    18→  const prefixes = [
    19→    "what is the meaning of ",
    20→    "what is the definition of ",
    21→    "what is the definition ",
    22→    "what is meant by ",
    23→    "what is ",
    24→    "what are ",
    25→    "what's ",
    26→    "define ",
    27→    "explain ",
    28→    "meaning of ",
    29→    "meaning ",
    30→    "can you explain ",
    31→    "please explain ",
    32→    "could you explain ",
    33→    "help me understand ",
    34→    "tell me about ",
    35→    "describe ",
    36→  ];
    37→  let topic = lower;
    38→  for (const prefix of prefixes) {
    39→    if (topic.startsWith(prefix)) {
    40→      topic = topic.slice(prefix.length);
    41→      break;
    42→    }
    43→  }
    44→  // Remove trailing question marks and "in simple terms" etc.
    45→  topic = topic.replace(/\?+.*$/, "").replace(/ in .+terms.*$/, "").replace(/ please.*$/, "").trim();
    46→  // Capitalize first letter for display
    47→  return topic.charAt(0).toUpperCase() + topic.slice(1);
    48→}

   334→
   335→## What Is a Verb?
   336→
   337→A **verb** is a word that describes an action, an event, or a state of being. Verbs are the engines that drive sentences — without a verb, a sentence cannot express what is happening, what has happened, or what will happen. Every complete sentence must contain at least one verb; it is the one part of speech that no sentence can do without.
   338→
   339→Verbs tell us what the subject of a sentence is doing (action verbs), what is happening to it (passive verbs), or what state it is in (linking verbs). They also carry crucial information about time (tense), completion (aspect), and who is performing the action (voice).
   340→
   341→## Types of Verbs
   342→
   343→### 1. Action Verbs (Dynamic Verbs)
   344→Action verbs describe what someone or something does — a physical or mental activity that can be observed or imagined.
   345→
   346→Examples: run, eat, study, write, jump, think, calculate, build, sing, dance
   347→
   348→In Zambia: The farmer **plants** maize. The student **reads** the textbook. The driver **steers** the bus along the Great East Road. The footballer **scores** a goal for Chipolopolo.
   349→
   350→### 2. Linking Verbs (State Verbs)
   351→Linking verbs do not express action. Instead, they connect the subject to additional information about the subject — a description, identity, or state. The most common linking verb is "be" (am, is, are, was, were, been, being), but others include: seem, become, appear, feel, look, sound, taste, remain, grow.
   352→
   353→Examples: Lusaka **is** the capital city. She **seems** tired. The water **becomes** cold at night. The market **looks** busy. The food **tastes** delicious.
   354→
   355→Linking verbs are like bridges — they do not show action, they show a relationship between the subject and its description.
   356→
   357→### 3. Helping Verbs (Auxiliary Verbs)
   358→Helping verbs work alongside a main verb to express tense, mood, or voice. They "help" the main verb by adding grammatical information.
   359→
   360→Common helping verbs: will, shall, can, may, must, should, would, could, might, has, have, had, do, does, did, is, am, are, was, were, been, being
   361→
   362→Example: She **will study** for her ECZ exams. (will = helping verb, study = main verb)
   363→Example: They **have completed** the assignment. (have = helping verb, completed = main verb)
   364→Example: The book **was written** by Mr. Banda. (was = helping verb, written = main verb — passive voice)
   365→
   366→### 4. Modal Verbs
   367→Modal verbs are a special type of helping verb that express ability, possibility, permission, or obligation.
   368→
   369→- **can/could:** ability or possibility — "She can speak three languages."
   370→- **may/might:** permission or possibility — "You may enter the classroom."
   371→- **must:** obligation or necessity — "Students must attend all ECZ exams."
   372→- **shall/should:** intention or recommendation — "You should study every day."
   373→- **will/would:** future intention or conditional — "I will help you with homework."

   381→- **Simple Past:** I **studied** yesterday. (completed action)

   383→

   386→- **Past Continuous:** I **was studying** when the phone rang. (action in progress in the past)
   387→- **Future Continuous:** I **will be studying** at 8 PM. (action in progress in the future)
   388→
   389→### Perfect Tenses
   390→- **Present Perfect:** I **have studied** all the chapters. (action completed at some point before now, with relevance to now)
   391→- **Past Perfect:** I **had studied** before the exam started. (action completed before another past action)

   653→
   654→Photosynthesis is one of the most important biological processes on Earth.    657→

   659→

   661→

   663→Sunlight provides the energy that drives the entire process. The light energy is captured by a green pigment called **chlorophyll**, which is found inside the chloroplasts of plant cells.    664→

   666→

   668→Water is absorbed by the plant's roots from the soil and transported upward through the stem to the leaves via specialized tubes called **xylem vessels**.    669→

   671→

   674→

   757→## Introduction

   759→Fractions are one of the most fundamental concepts in mathematics.    762→
   763→## What Is a Fraction?
   764→
   765→A fraction represents a part of a whole. It is written as two numbers separated by a line (or slash):
   766→
   767→**Numerator / Denominator**
   768→
   769→- **Numerator** (top number): How many parts you have or are considering
   770→- **Denominator** (bottom number): Total number of equal parts the whole is divided into
   771→
   772→Example: If you cut a mango into 4 equal pieces and eat 1 piece, you ate **1/4** of the mango. The numerator (1) tells you you ate one piece. The denominator (4) tells you the mango was divided into four equal pieces.
   773→

   775→
   776→## Types of Fractions
   777→
   778→### 1. Proper Fractions
   779→The numerator is smaller than the denominator. The value is less than 1.
   780→
   781→Examples: 1/4, 2/5, 3/8, 7/10
   782→
   783→In context: If you walk **3/4** of the way to school, you have not yet arrived (less than the whole distance).
   784→

   786→The numerator is equal to or greater than the denominator. The value is 1 or more.
   787→
   788→Examples: 5/4, 7/3, 12/5, 8/8 (= 1 exactly)
   789→

   791→

   793→A whole number combined with a proper fraction. This is another way to write improper fractions.
   794→
   795→Examples: 1 and 1/4 (same as 5/4), 2 and 1/3 (same as 7/3), 3 and 2/5 (same as 17/5)
   796→
   797→Converting between improper fractions and mixed numbers:
   798→- Improper to mixed: Divide numerator by denominator. 5 ÷ 4 = 1 remainder 1, so 5/4 = 1 and 1/4
   799→- Mixed to improper: Multiply the whole number by the denominator, add the numerator, put over denominator. 1 and 1/4 = (1 × 4 + 1)/4 = 5/4
   800→

   802→Fractions that look different but represent the same value. You create equivalent fractions by multiplying or dividing both numerator and denominator by the same number.
   803→
   804→Examples: 1/2 = 2/4 = 3/6 = 4/8 = 5/10 = 50/100
   805→
   806→This works because multiplying both parts by the same number is like cutting each piece into smaller sub-pieces — the total amount stays the same.

   809→

   811→

   813→
   814→1/4 + 2/4 = 3/4 (one quarter plus two quarters equals three quarters)
   815→
   816→Think of it literally: if you have one quarter of a mango and someone gives you two more quarters, you now have three quarters of a mango.
   817→

   819→

   823→

   825→

   827→
